Projects

Description: This project involved a comprehensive analysis of a dataset containing over 2.1 million vehicle collisions in New York City since 2012. The goal was to uncover patterns and identify key areas of interest related to collisions caused by factors such as poor road conditions, driving under the influence, adverse weather, and general hazards. To achieve this, I employed K-Means Clustering to group collision data into distinct clusters based on these contributing factors.

The project began with extensive data cleaning, where I wrote a program using regular expressions to correct common misspellings and inconsistencies in the raw dataset. Additionally, I utilized APIs for geolocation and reverse geolocation to address missing values in the spatial data. Significant feature engineering was carried out, including creating features related to holidays, discretizing time series data (e.g., day of the week, week/month of the year), and integrating weather and vehicle information from related datasets.

I also visualized the data to identify and highlight trends and patterns, providing valuable insights into collision hotspots and contributing factors. This analysis aimed to enhance understanding of collision dynamics and support targeted interventions to improve road safety.

Technologies Used: Python, Pandas, Scikit-learn (K-Means Clustering), Regular Expressions, APIs (for geolocation), Matplotlib, Seaborn, Jupyter Notebooks.

Outcome/Impact: The project provided actionable insights into high-risk areas and contributing factors for vehicle collisions in NYC. The findings were instrumental in identifying patterns related to road conditions, driver behavior, and weather impacts, which could be used to inform policy decisions and improve road safety measures. The data cleaning and feature engineering processes also significantly enhanced the quality and usability of the dataset for future analyses.
